CHAPTER 11

Key Takeaways

  • OBV detects hidden market coils

    Why it matters: Volume often precedes price. Rising OBV during a flat price trend signals hidden accumulation.

    Evidence: Granville's observation that OBV highs/lows reliably precede price breakouts. Ch 11, p. 148

    Application: Watch for divergences where OBV breaks out before price does.

    Caveat: OBV treats a 1-cent gain exactly the same as a 10-dollar gain.

  • VPT improves upon OBV by adding magnitude

    Why it matters: Weighting volume by the percentage price change creates a more proportional measure of supply/demand.

    Evidence: Markstein's 1966 adaptation integrates price magnitude directly into the volume flow. Ch 11, p. 150

    Application: Use VPT instead of OBV when tracking highly volatile assets where daily price ranges vary wildly.

    Caveat: VPT can be distorted by sudden massive gap-ups on earnings.

  • Rolling vs. Continuous Accumulation

    Why it matters: While Granville insisted on continuous lifetime running totals, rolling periods adapt faster to current conditions.

    Evidence: Modern practitioners often use rolling 20-period OBV. Ch 11, p. 148

    Application: Apply a 20-period rolling OBV for short-term swing trading to filter out historical baggage.

    Caveat: Granville firmly opposed rolling totals for long-term forecasting.

  • VZO turns accumulation into an oscillator

    Why it matters: Oscillators provide clear overbought/oversold boundaries.

    Evidence: Khalil bounded the VZO between -100 and +100 by dividing EMA of +/- volume by EMA of total volume. Ch 11, p. 150

    Application: Use VZO to identify exhaustion points in cumulative volume trends.

    Caveat: Oscillators can stay "overbought" for long periods during strong trends.

CHAPTER 11

Context & Background

1. Intellectual Tradition

  • School of Thought: Cumulative Volume Analysis.
  • Intellectual Lineage: Paul Clay (1932) → Frank Vignolia (1946) → Joe Granville (1961) → David Markstein (1966).
  • Key Influences: Granville brought OBV to the mainstream, turning technical analysis from a niche charting practice into a popular strategy.

2. Historical Moment

  • Era: Mid-20th Century (1960s).
  • Technological State: Pre-computer charting. OBV was revolutionary because it was mathematically simple enough to calculate by hand daily (just add or subtract volume).

3. Evolution of Ideas

  • Then (1960s): OBV was a groundbreaking insight into hidden institutional accumulation.
  • Now (2026): Algorithms can easily mask their volume footprints intraday, yet interday accumulation remains a robust macro-indicator. Variants like VZO apply modern smoothing (EMA) and oscillation techniques impossible to do quickly by hand in 1961.

CHAPTER 11

Mental Models: On-Balance Volume (OBV)

Core Principle: Volume on an "up" day represents pure demand; volume on a "down" day represents pure supply. Accumulating them over time reveals the true balance of power.

How It Works:

  1. If today's close > yesterday's close: add today's volume to the running total.
  2. If today's close < yesterday's close: subtract today's volume from the running total.
  3. If unchanged: do nothing.

When to Apply:

  • Consolidations: When price is moving sideways in a tight range.
  • Trend Confirmation: To verify if a price trend has the participation required to sustain it.

Limitations:

  • Binary Flaw: A close up by 1 cent adds the entire day's volume as positive, treating it identically to a +$10 day.
  • Starting Point Arbitrariness: The absolute number of OBV is meaningless; only its trend matters.
CHAPTER 11

Mental Models: VPT & VZO

Volume Price Trend (VPT)

Core Principle: Volume accumulation should be proportional to the magnitude of the price move.

How It Works: VPT = Prior VPT + (Current Volume × % Change in Close). This solves OBV's binary flaw. A massive up-day heavily impacts the indicator; a flat up-day barely moves it.

Volume Zone Oscillator (VZO)

Core Principle: Cumulative volume trends experience exhaustion and can be mapped within overbought/oversold bounds.

How It Works: Smooths +/- volume with an EMA, divides by the EMA of total volume, and multiplies by 100. Range is fixed from -100 to +100.

Application: Identifying shorter-term mean-reversion setups within the broader volume trend. When VZO hits +90, buying pressure may be temporarily exhausted.

Deep Dive: Mechanics of Interday Accumulation

1. Core Argument

By classifying entire daily volumes as either "buying" or "selling" based purely on the closing price, and aggregating them over time, you filter out market noise and expose the underlying flow of funds.

2. Logic Structure

  • Premise 1: Net buying pressure results in a higher close; net selling results in a lower close.
  • Premise 2: Institutional participation leaves a massive volume footprint.
  • Conclusion: A cumulative ledger of volume signed by closing direction accurately tracks institutional positioning over time.

3. Unstated Assumptions

  • Assumes the closing price accurately reflects the day's true balance of power (ignoring intraday reversals).
  • Assumes all volume traded on an "up" day can be meaningfully classified as demand.

Reading Layers: Deconstructing Interday Volume

  • Layer 1: Surface Reading
    OBV adds volume on up days and subtracts on down days to show supply/demand trends. VPT improves this by adding percentage change.
  • Layer 2: Structural Reading
    The author structures the chapter as an evolutionary timeline: starting with OBV's blunt binary logic (Granville), advancing to VPT's proportional logic (Markstein), and culminating in VZO's modern oscillatory logic (Khalil).
  • Layer 3: Contextual Reading
    These indicators reflect an era where daily closing data was the primary unit of analysis. They are "interday" because historical analysts couldn't easily access tick-by-tick intraday data.
  • Layer 4: Critical Reading
    The binary nature of OBV is fundamentally flawed in modern, highly volatile markets where a stock might open down 5%, rally 4.9% intraday, and close down 0.1%. OBV would register the entire massive accumulation day as negative volume.
  • Layer 5: Generative Reading
    How can we merge OBV logic with modern data? We could create an "Intraday-Weighted OBV" that splits daily volume precisely at the transaction level, or applies the VZO oscillator framework to high-frequency tick data.

Evidence Evaluation

Evidence Type: Expert Authority (Joe Granville) / Logical Argument

Quality Score: ★★★☆☆ (3/5)

Strengths:

  • Logically sound: Price cannot sustain a move without capital, which manifests as volume.
  • Empirically validated over decades of charting history; OBV divergences are standard tools.

Weaknesses:

  • Chapter lacks statistical backtesting (e.g., win rates of OBV breakouts). Relies on Granville's authority.
  • Vulnerable to gap-risk: Earnings gaps can drastically distort the OBV line permanently.

2026 Update:

In modern algo-driven markets, large block trades are executed in dark pools or sliced into VWAP algorithms, intentionally designed to hide from indicators like OBV. Yet, because algorithms still must eventually move the closing price to find liquidity, macro-level OBV trends remain structurally valid for swing traders.

CHAPTER 11

Competing Views: The Time Horizon Debate

Granville's Stance (Continuous OBV): OBV must be kept as a continuous running total from the inception of the asset. Ch 11, p. 148

Alternative View: Rolling OBV (Modern Quants)

Core Claim: Accumulation from 10 years ago has zero predictive value for tomorrow's price. OBV should be restricted to a rolling period (e.g., 20 or 50 days).

Why They Differ: Granville viewed OBV as an absolute structural ledger of supply/demand. Modern quants view it as a momentum state that decays over time.

Synthesis & Integration

  • When Granville is right: Analyzing long-term macro cycles in major indices (like the S&P 500) where long-term demographics and secular shifts matter.
  • When Rolling OBV is right: Active swing trading, where recent institutional positioning (the last 30 days) is the only relevant driver of near-term liquidity.

  • Add OBV and VPT to your charting platform below price.
  • Look back at the last 5 major breakouts on your favorite stock. Did OBV make a new high before the price did?

Phase 2: Experimentation (Week 2-4)

  • The Setup: Scan for stocks whose price has been in a tight 3-week trading range, but whose OBV line is trending upward at a 45-degree angle.
  • The Action: Take small position sizes upon the actual price breakout, using the OBV divergence as your primary conviction filter.

Phase 3: Integration (Month 2+)

  • Compare OBV and VPT on the same charts. Notice how VPT filters out the noise of low-volatility, low-range days.
  • Test rolling 20-period OBV vs continuous OBV to see which fits your trading timeframe best.

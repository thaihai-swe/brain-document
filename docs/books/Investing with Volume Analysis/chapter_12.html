CH 12 · THE BIG IDEA

The Flaw of Accumulation

CH 12 · THE BIG IDEA
"Intraday indicators quantify daily buying pressure, but summing these short-term interactions over long periods can produce flawed, lagging signals."
While useful for bar-to-bar chart reading, intraday volume changes shouldn't be accumulated over time.
CH 12 · KEY TAKEAWAYS

Important Lessons

  • The Close Dictates Intraday Conviction
    Why it matters: The close relative to the high/low reveals whether buyers or sellers controlled the final hours and are willing to hold overnight. Evidence: Bostian's Intraday Intensity Index (1967) uses the close's position to assign positive/negative volume. Ch 12, p. 133 Application: Prioritize closes near the daily high when looking for bullish continuation. Caveat: Only useful for a few bars; accumulating this metric historically distorts the current trend.
  • "Tweeners" Solve the Gap Problem
    Why it matters: Standard intraday indicators ignore overnight gaps, missing huge chunks of price action. Evidence: Wilder's True Range incorporates the previous day's close to account for gaps. Ch 12, p. 134 Application: Use Twiggs' Money Flow over Chaikin's because Twiggs uses True Range to bridge the interday/intraday gap. Caveat: Even true range indicators are subject to the flaws of long-term accumulation.
  • Persistency of Money Flow Enhances Signals
    Why it matters: Sustained buying pressure is more reliable than a single volatile day. Evidence: Chaikin noted that if Money Flow stays above zero for 9 out of 10 periods, the trend is robust. Ch 12, p. 136 Application: Don't just look at the raw oscillator value; measure how many days it stays positive. Caveat: Overextended persistency can indicate overbought conditions ripe for reversal.
  • Effort vs. Results is Strictly Short-Term
    Why it matters: The core failure of intraday accumulation is extrapolating micro-forces into macro-trends. Evidence: Dormeier agrees with Granville that summing intraday changes over time is conceptually flawed. Ch 12, p. 139 Application: Restrict intraday volume analysis to 3-5 bar forecasting rather than macro trend following. Caveat: Requires distinct interday indicators for long-term investing.
CH 12 · MENTAL MODELS

The Tweener & Persistency

Model 1: The "Tweener" Gap Bridge

Core Principle: A purely intraday indicator misses overnight news and gaps, while a purely interday indicator misses intraday volatility. The "Tweener" bridges this.

How It Works: By substituting the daily High/Low with Wilder's True High/True Low (which includes yesterday's close), the indicator captures the entire price journey, including overnight gaps.

When to Apply: - Evaluating earnings gap-ups
- Markets with heavy overnight futures trading
- Swing trading where overnight risk is a primary factor

Limitations: It makes the formula mathematically heavier and blurs the strict definition of "intraday" vs "interday".

Model 2: Persistency of Pressure

Core Principle: The duration of money flow is more predictive than the magnitude of a single day's flow.

How It Works: Count the percentage of time an oscillator remains above its zero line over a rolling window (e.g., 90% persistency over 10 days).

Example from Chapter: Postulated as the secret sauce behind IBD's CANSLIM supply/demand ratings. Ch 12, p. 137

CH 12 · CONTEXT & BACKGROUND

The 1975 Data Crisis

1. Historical Moment & Data Evolution

  • The 1975 Data Crisis: Larry Williams built WVAD around the "Open" price. But in 1975, financial publications stopped printing opening prices to save space.
  • The Workaround: This forced Williams to adopt Wilder's True Range, accidentally creating a more robust "tweener" indicator out of necessity rather than design.

2. Intellectual Lineage

  • The Pioneers: David Bostian (1967) → Larry Williams (1972) → Marc Chaikin (1975) → Colin Twiggs.
  • The Divide: Granville and Dormeier stand on the side of "Interday" accumulation (Close-to-Close). Bostian, Williams, and Chaikin championed "Intraday" accumulation.

3. Modern Relevance (2026)

  • Then: Missing the "Open" in newspapers was a severe handicap.
  • Now: Tick-by-tick data is ubiquitous. We can calculate WVAD effortlessly, yet True Range derivatives (like Twiggs' Money Flow) remain superior because they logically account for the massive overnight gap action caused by algorithmic trading and 24-hour global news cycles.
CH 12 · NOTABLE QUOTES

Granville's Warning

"When I asked Joe Granville his opinion of accumulating intraday changes as opposed to the closing changes data, he emphatically stated, 'They're dead wrong.'"

— Buff Dormeier recounting his conversation with Joe Granville Ch 12, p. 139

Interpretation: Granville, the father of On-Balance Volume, completely dismisses the foundational premise of Chaikin and Williams. He believes that only close-to-close changes reflect true market accumulation.

Significance: It highlights a major schism in technical analysis. Dormeier sides with Granville conceptually, arguing that Wyckoff's "effort vs results" is a micro-phenomenon that loses meaning when summed into a macro-indicator over months or years.

Application: Stop using Chaikin Money Flow for long-term trend analysis. Relegate it to short-term, bar-to-bar tactical trading.

CH 12 · DEEP DIVE

The Mathematics of Intraday Pressure

Deep Dive 1: How Intraday Indicators Actually Work

1. The Close-to-Range Ratio

Intraday indicators live and die by a single mathematical assumption: Where a stock closes within its daily high-low range reveals institutional intent.

2. Bostian's Intraday Intensity (1967)

Formula: (2 * Close - High - Low) / (High - Low) * Volume

If a stock has a range of $10, and it closes at the absolute high, the multiplier is +1. The entire day's volume is added to the running total. If it closes exactly in the middle, the multiplier is 0. No volume is added. If it closes at the low, the multiplier is -1. The volume is subtracted.

3. The Fatal Flaw of the Multiplier

If a stock gaps down $50 on terrible earnings, but closes at the very top of its new, lower intraday range, Bostian's formula records a massive accumulation of positive volume. The indicator screams "Buy!" while the stock is crashing.

This is why Wilder's True Range (which includes the gap) became mandatory to fix these formulas.

CH 12 · DEEP DIVE

Why Accumulation Breaks Down

Deep Dive 2: Why Accumulation Breaks Down

1. The Core Argument

Dormeier argues that intraday relationships (like a strong close) are predictive for the next 2-3 bars, but summing them into an oscillator over 21, 50, or 200 days creates a mathematically invalid indicator.

2. Logical Structure

  • Premise 1: Intraday "effort vs results" measures micro-supply and demand.
  • Premise 2: Micro-dynamics dictate immediate, short-term price reactions.
  • Conclusion: Accumulating these micro-dynamics over months implies that yesterday's strong intraday close still matters 60 days later. Dormeier asserts this is logically false.

3. The "Memory" Problem

Accumulation indicators have infinite memory (or 21-period memory for oscillators). A massive volume spike 20 days ago heavily distorts Chaikin Money Flow today, even if the current trend has shifted. By focusing on the intraday location rather than the actual price change, the indicator decouples from the primary trend.

CH 12 · READING LAYERS

Decoding the Subtext

Reading Layers

Layer 1: Surface Reading

The chapter lists several historical volume indicators (WVAD, WAD, Chaikin, Twiggs) that use intraday price action instead of close-to-close price action, explaining their formulas and standard interpretations.

Layer 2: Structural Reading

Dormeier deliberately structures this chapter as a historical progression of an idea he ultimately rejects. He shows the evolution from Bostian to Twiggs, acknowledges their utility for short-term trading, and then drops the hammer at the end: they shouldn't be accumulated over time.

Layer 4: Critical Reading

Dormeier critiques the entire sub-field of intraday accumulation. However, a weakness in his critique is that he dismisses them conceptually without providing statistical backtesting to prove that Chaikin Money Flow underperforms OBV over long periods.

Layer 5: Generative Reading

If intraday data is only good for the short term, can we use a dual-timeframe model? Use OBV for the macro trend filter, and Twiggs Money Flow strictly as a 3-day trigger mechanism for entries and exits.

CH 12 · EVIDENCE EVALUATION

Assessing the Persistency of Money Flow

Evidence Evaluation: The Persistency of Money Flow

Claim: Persistency of Money Flow (staying above zero for 9 of 10 periods) is a robust indicator of supply and demand, potentially used by IBD's CANSLIM.

Evidence Type: Anecdotal / Historical Observation

Quality Score: ★★☆☆☆ (2/5)

Strengths:

  • Logically sound: sustained buying pressure over 10 days is harder to manipulate than a single day's volume spike.

Weaknesses:

  • Dormeier provides no statistical evidence, win rates, or broad market studies to validate that >90% persistency leads to alpha.
  • The link to IBD's CANSLIM is stated as "postulated," meaning it is a rumor or reverse-engineering guess, not verified fact.

2026 Context:

Modern quantitative analysis easily backtests Chaikin Money Flow persistency. The consensus is that while high persistency indicates strong momentum, it often coincides with mean-reversion tops. Buying immediately after 10 days of positive CMF usually yields negative short-term expected returns due to exhaustion.

CH 12 · COMPETING VIEWS

Granville vs. Chaikin

Competing Views: The Accumulation Debate

This Chapter's Position (Dormeier/Granville):

Wyckoff's "effort vs results" (intraday price/volume action) only matters for the immediate future. Summing intraday changes into long-term accumulation indicators is a conceptual error.

Alternative View (Marc Chaikin / Larry Williams):

Core Claim: The close-to-close change (used by OBV) masks true institutional intent. Institutions accumulate quietly intraday, closing the stock strong while keeping interday price flat. Accumulating intraday intensity is the ONLY way to see the "smart money" footprint over time.

Why They Differ:

  • Granville believes price dictates reality: if the stock didn't close higher than yesterday, there was no real progress, regardless of intraday games.
  • Chaikin believes the intraday mechanics reveal hidden pressure before the price eventually breaks out.

Synthesis:

Use Chaikin/Twiggs to spot hidden accumulation in tight consolidation bases (where interday price is flat). Once the trend breaks out, switch to OBV or VPCI to measure macro trend health.

CH 12 · IMPLEMENTATION

Trading the Tweener

Implementation Guide: Trading the "Tweener"

Phase 1: Setup (Week 1)

  • Remove standard Chaikin Money Flow from your charts.
  • Replace it with Twiggs' Money Flow (21-period), as it properly handles overnight gaps using True Range.

Phase 2: Experimentation (Week 2-4)

  • Small-scale test: Look for divergences. When price makes a new high but Twiggs' Money Flow makes a lower high, flag the stock.
  • Track results: Do these divergences lead to short-term corrections within the next 3-5 bars?

Phase 3: Integration (Month 2+)

  • Implement Dormeier's rule: Do not use Twiggs for long-term macro trend following.
  • Use it specifically for bar-to-bar chart reading. If you are entering a swing trade today, use Twiggs to confirm intraday accumulation is supporting your entry.

Common Pitfall:

Ignoring a massive gap-down because Twiggs' Money Flow ticked upward. Never let an intraday indicator override the reality of absolute price destruction.

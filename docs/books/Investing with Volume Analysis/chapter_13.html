Price range volume indicators act as the tape measures of market action, quantifying precisely how much volume (effort) was expended to achieve the price movement (result).

CH 13 · CONTEXT & BACKGROUND

Evolution of Range Indicators

1. Author's Lens & Evolution of Ideas

The concepts in Chapter 13 represent a synthesis of several generations of technical thought. The author traces the lineage from Edwin S. Quinn's Trendographs in the 1930s (applauded by H.M. Gartley) to modern applications by Richard Arms and Bill Williams.

2. Historical & Intellectual Tradition

  • The 1930s Tape Readers: Early attempts to visually integrate volume and price range without modern computing power.
  • Richard Arms (Equivolume & EoM): Shifted the paradigm in the 1970s/80s by declaring that the market should be "divided into shares, not hours."
  • Bill Williams (MFI - 1995): Systematized range/volume into four explicit "Profitunity Windows", adding a behavioral finance overlay.

3. The 2026 Perspective

While originally designed for daily charting and human visual processing, these mathematical relationships (like MFI and EoM) are now embedded directly into multi-factor algorithmic trading models as quantitative features.

CH 13 · DEEP DIVE

Effort vs. Result Quantified

1. Core Argument

Price range volume indicators are the "tape measures" of the market. They don't just ask what happened to the price, but how hard it was to make it happen. They are fundamentally based on Wyckoff's law of Effort vs. Result.

2. Logical Structure: The Physics of Price

The logic mirrors basic physics: Force = Mass × Acceleration.

  • Effort (Volume): The raw force or fuel being burned.
  • Result (Price Range): The distance the asset traveled.
  • Efficiency (MFI / EoM): How easily the asset moves per unit of volume. A high MFI means the market is a vacuum; price glides easily. A low MFI means high friction.

3. Practical Implications

By measuring the friction (Squat bars or low Ease of Movement), traders can anticipate exhaustion before price actually reverses. The divergence between effort and result is the leading indicator.

CH 13 · MENTAL MODELS

Market Facilitation Index (MFI)

Market Facilitation Index (MFI)

A measure of market efficiency: MFI = (High - Low) / Volume Ch 13, p. 160
How It Works:
  • MFI measures how many points the price moved per unit of volume.
  • It is analyzed by comparing today's MFI and Volume against yesterday's, creating four "Profitunity Windows".
⚠️ Limitations & Caveats:
  • Requires accurate volume data (can be noisy in fragmented crypto/forex markets).
  • Bar-to-bar analysis is short-term; not meant for long-term investing trends.

CH 13 · MENTAL MODELS

Equivolume & Ease of Movement

Equivolume & The Power Box

Replaces the time X-axis with volume. Ch 13, p. 161
The Power Box:

A price bar that is both exceptionally tall (wide range) and exceptionally wide (high volume).

When a Power Box breaks through support or resistance, it confirms institutional conviction. It is the visual equivalent of a Green (++) MFI.

Ease of Movement (EoM)

Midpoint Move / Box Ratio. Ch 13, p. 164
How it works:
  • Midpoint: Directional change of the bar's center.
  • Box Ratio: Vol / Range.

High EoM = Price moves up easily on low volume. Low EoM = Price falls easily on light volume. Near zero = Heavy volume friction.

Wright's 407% MFI Returns

📊 Backtest Data (Charley F. Wright)

The Claim: Using MFI Green ++ bars on 30-minute S&P 500 futures, Wright generated a 407% return and 1.49 profit factor between 1986 and 1988 holding overnight, and 312% with a 2.12 profit factor not holding overnight. Ch 13, p. 161

Weaknesses:

  • Time Period: 1986-1988 is a very small, specific sample size featuring the '87 crash.
  • Survivorship & Selection Bias: We do not have insight into the exact entry/exit stops used in this "nonoptimized" system.

2026 Update: While absolute returns of 400%+ over 2 years are rarely reproducible in today's high-frequency markets, the Profit Factor (1.49 - 2.12) is a realistic target for robust modern algo systems utilizing volume/range expansion features.

CH 13 · COUNTERARGUMENTS

The 2026 Algorithmic Critique

The 2026 Algorithmic Critique

While the logic of MFI, Equivolume, and EoM is structurally sound, modern market microstructure presents distinct counterarguments:

1. Intraday Range Compression (The Algo Effect)

Equities today experience significant volume spikes at the Open and Close (MOC orders), with hollow midday trading. Applying MFI or EoM without normalizing for the U-shaped intraday volume curve produces massive false "Squat" and "Fake" signals.

2. Dark Pools & Hidden Volume

In 2026, ~40-50% of equity volume can execute off-exchange (Dark Pools). The Result (Price Range) is printed on lit exchanges, but the Effort (Volume) is fragmented. This can artificially inflate MFI (making the market look more efficient than it is) because the denominator (lit volume) is under-reported.

Synthesis:

These indicators remain powerful, but the raw inputs must be scrubbed. Volume must be normalized against a rolling time-of-day baseline, and off-exchange volume estimates should be incorporated for a true picture of "Effort".

CH 13 · READING LAYERS

Five Depths of Volume Analysis

Five Depths of Range-Volume Analysis

  • Layer 1: Mechanical Formula

    MFI = Range / Volume. Calculating the raw ratios.

  • Layer 2: Signal Generation

    Categorizing the data into discrete windows (Green, Fade, Fake, Squat) or Power Boxes.

  • Layer 3: The Physics of Friction

    Understanding that these formulas are proxies for physical resistance. High volume + low range = hitting a brick wall of limit orders.

  • Layer 4: Institutional Psychology

    Recognizing the "Fake" bar (-+) as smart money distributing into retail FOMO. The wider range is bait; the low volume proves lack of institutional sponsorship.

  • Layer 5: Quantitative Feature Engineering

    Transforming Arms' and Williams' visual logic into normalized, continuous data streams (z-scores of EoM) to feed machine learning models.

Integrating Range Indicators into Your Workflow

Step 1: Change your charting software to display Equivolume candles instead of standard Japanese Candlesticks for one week. This breaks the habit of treating time as a constant.

Step 2: Add the Market Facilitation Index (MFI) histogram to the bottom of your chart. Color-code the bars (Green, Blue/Fade, Pink/Fake, Red/Squat).

Step 3: Screen for "Squat" bars at major support/resistance levels. When you see high volume but a compressed range, put the asset on a watchlist for an imminent breakout or sharp reversal within 3-5 periods.

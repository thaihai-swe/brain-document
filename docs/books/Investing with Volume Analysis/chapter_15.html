CH 15 · THE BIG IDEA

The Fall of the Tick

CH 15 · THE BIG IDEA
"Tick-based volume attempts to measure conviction trade-by-trade, but modern algorithmic execution and decimalization have fractured its reliability, forcing a shift to broader volume-weighted indicators."
What was once the purest electronic tape reading has been rendered opaque by institutional stealth tactics.
CH 15 · KEY TAKEAWAYS

Important Lessons

  • Every Tick Counts (Theoretically)
    Why it matters: Tick volume inherently assigns a buyer/seller label to every transaction by seeing if it occurred on an uptick or downtick, precisely calculating supply vs. demand. Evidence: The Money Flow calculation strictly accumulates (Tick Price × Uptick Volume) - (Tick Price × Downtick Volume). Ch 15, p. 153 Application: Useful conceptually to understand intraday liquidity and tape reading mechanics. Caveat: Highly susceptible to modern market-making noise and scalper inventories.
  • Institutional Footprints via Block Trades
    Why it matters: Tracking block (>10k shares) vs. nonblock trades was once the prime method for seeing if "smart money" was accumulating while retail was distributing. Evidence: Birinyi's parsing of block Money Flow tracks whether large institutions are positioning ahead of price moves. Ch 15, p. 154 Application: Observe divergences—if price falls but block flow is positive, institutions are buying weakness. Caveat: Today, institutions chop 100k blocks into 100-share algorithmic lots, hiding their tracks entirely.
  • The Decimalization Shock
    Why it matters: Moving from fractional pricing to pennies destroyed the predictive power of traditional tick accumulation by creating endless, meaningless micro-ticks. Evidence: The author notes decimalization strongly reduced the reliability of intraday analysis. Ch 15, p. 155 Application: Do not rely on raw uptick/downtick accumulation on 1-minute or tick charts in the 2020s. Caveat: Alternative theories like Willain's Effective Volume are required to decode modern data.
CH 15 · MENTAL MODELS

Tick Flow & Block Divergence

Mental Models

Model 1: The Tick-Flow Accumulation Engine

Core Principle: A trade on an uptick is driven by aggressive buyers lifting the offer. A trade on a downtick is driven by aggressive sellers hitting the bid. Adding these over time reveals the true balance of power.

How It Works: Multiply the volume of the trade by its price. If uptick, add to a running total. If downtick, subtract. The resulting curve is the "Money Flow."

Limitations: Assumes a clear dichotomy between active buyers and active sellers, which falls apart when market makers fill orders from existing inventory in sub-penny increments.

Model 2: The Smart/Dumb Money Divergence

Core Principle: Large size equals institutional intelligence; small size equals retail reaction.

How It Works: Separate the tick data into sizes >10,000 shares (blocks) and <10,000 shares (nonblocks). Compare their trajectories.

When to Apply (Historically): To confirm trends. If price is rising but block flow turns negative, the "smart money" is secretly dumping shares to euphoric retail buyers.

CH 15 · CONTEXT & BACKGROUND

The Demise of the Tape

Context & Background: The Demise of the Tape

1. Historical Moment
The concepts in this chapter bridge the 1990s (the peak of Birinyi's Money Flow dominance) and the 2000s (the onset of decimalization and HFT). Prior to 2001, US stocks traded in fractions (1/16th of a dollar, or 6.25 cents). A price change required significant conviction to cross that spread. Decimalization shrank the spread to 1 penny, turning significant ticks into random algorithmic noise.

2. Evolution of Ideas
- 1950s: Don Worden develops "Tick Volume", discovering an edge in tracking trade-by-trade imbalances.
- 1990s: Laszlo Birinyi popularizes block vs. nonblock Money Flow, essentially computerizing Jesse Livermore's legendary tape reading.
- Post-2000s: Institutional trading becomes 90% of the market. Algorithms slice blocks into tiny pieces, masking institutional intent. Worden abandons the metric.
- Today (2026): Raw tick accumulation is largely abandoned in favor of smoothed volume-weighted price metrics.

3. Author's Lens
The author admits to initially aligning with Birinyi's optimistic view of Money Flow. However, clear-eyed realism about market mechanics (decimalization, scalping, algorithmic execution) forced the author to recognize the indicator's obsolescence in its raw form.

CH 15 · NOTABLE QUOTES

Birinyi and Worden

"It’s the most useful indicator that exists, because it tells you what people are doing while they are doing it."

— Laszlo Birinyi (August 1993 Bloomberg article), referring to Money Flow. Ch 15, p. 155

Interpretation: Birinyi viewed Money Flow as the ultimate x-ray into market sentiment. Unlike lagging price indicators, Money Flow promised real-time visibility into whether the underlying pressure was accumulation or distribution.

"Originally, it had a phenomenal ability to contradict immediate price trends... However, as the market gradually went from 90 percent publicly dominated to 90 percent institutionally dominated, the large transaction developed an overwhelming negative bias."

— Don Worden, explaining why he abandoned Tick Volume. Ch 15, p. 155

Significance: Worden's observation highlights a critical truth in technical analysis: market structure changes can permanently destroy an indicator's edge. As institutions became the entire market, the baseline assumptions of the indicator failed.
CH 15 · DEEP DIVE

The Breakdown of an Indicator

Deep Dive: The Breakdown of an Indicator

1. The Core Argument
Trade-by-trade tick volume was once the purest reflection of supply and demand, but technological shifts have rendered it effectively obsolete for retail traders.

2. The Logical Structure
The original theory of Money Flow relied on a clean deductive chain:
Premise 1: Trades executed on upticks represent aggressive demand (buying).
Premise 2: Trades of 10,000+ shares represent institutional capital.
Conclusion: A rising accumulation of large uptick volume perfectly mirrors institutional accumulation.

3. The Fatal Flaws (Modern Counterexamples)
- Algorithmic Execution: Institutions now "work" their blocks via VWAP algorithms, selling into upticks at the offer and buying into downticks at the bid to stay hidden. Ch 15, p. 155
- Sub-Penny Routing: High-frequency trading (HFT) and dark pools mean that public tape ticks often represent arbitrage and inventory balancing by market makers, not genuine directional conviction.

4. Practical Implications
Because institutions actively engineer their trades to look like the exact opposite of what they are doing (e.g., selling into strength to look like retail buying), raw Tick Volume often generates false signals in modern, highly liquid equities.

CH 15 · COMPETING VIEWS

Birinyi vs. Worden

Competing Views: Birinyi vs. Worden

View 1: Laszlo Birinyi (The Tape Reader)
Core Claim: Money flow is the ultimate real-time truth. Computerized tape reading allows you to see the exact supply and demand imbalances as they occur.
Context: Forged in an era where institutional footprints were heavy and undeniable.

View 2: Don Worden (The Pragmatist)
Core Claim: Tick volume lost its edge when the market ecosystem shifted. It developed an overwhelming negative bias and had to be abandoned.
Context: As the creator of the indicator, Worden recognized that a tool built for a 90% retail market broke when the market became 90% institutional.

Synthesis & Integration
Birinyi is correct in theory—if you could see perfect, unmanipulated order flow, it would be the holy grail. Worden is correct in practice—you cannot see unmanipulated order flow anymore. The author's solution is to abandon raw ticks and move to smoothed, volume-weighted metrics across broader trends.

CH 15 · EVIDENCE EVALUATION

Evaluating Block Divergence

Evidence Evaluation: Evaluating Block Divergence

Claim: "If block trades move up while total or nonblock Money Flow moves down, this suggests that institutions are position building, or accumulating." Ch 15, p. 154

Evidence Type: ☐ Empirical Data ☑ Logical/Historical Axiom

Quality Score: ⭐⭐☆☆☆ (2/5 in modern context)

Strengths:
- Conceptually flawless. Divergence between large capital and small capital has been a reliable market tell since the days of Richard Wyckoff.

Weaknesses:
- Defunct Categorization: The threshold of 10,000 shares to denote a "block" is arbitrary and hopelessly outdated. Today, a 100-share lot is just as likely to be a fragment of a multi-million dollar institutional iceberg order as it is a retail trader.

2026 Update (Relevance Today: Low):
The rise of Zero-DTE options, dark pools, and payment for order flow (PFOF) means the "block vs. nonblock" dichotomy on the public tape is completely broken. Modern quants use complex "toxic flow" models, not basic size thresholds, to determine institutional presence.

CH 15 · MODERN CONTEXT

Dark Pools & Algorithmic Slicing

Modern Context: The Impact of Dark Pools & Algorithmic Slicing (Deep Extrapolation)

To fully grasp why Tick Volume failed, we must extrapolate the author's brief mention of "decimalization" to its modern logical extreme in 2026.

1. Iceberg Orders & Time-Weighted Average Price (TWAP)
Institutions no longer drop 50,000 shares on the bid. Algorithms slice the 50,000 shares into 500 separate 100-share orders, executed randomly over 4 hours. On a tick chart, this looks like retail noise (nonblock), completely bypassing Birinyi's block filter.

2. Dark Pools & Off-Exchange Routing
Up to 40-50% of modern equity volume is executed off-exchange in dark pools or by internalizers (like Citadel). These trades are printed to the tape late, and out of sequence. Accumulating their "upticks" or "downticks" produces garbage data, as the tick direction at the time of the print does not reflect the market state at the time of execution.

3. The Solution: Effective Volume
As the author notes, Pascal Willain's Effective Volume attempts to solve this by analyzing minute-by-minute volume surges that exceed expected thresholds, seeking the hidden "elephants" rather than relying on raw trade sizes.

CH 15 · READING LAYERS

Deconstructing Tick Volume

Reading Layers: Deconstructing Tick Volume

Layer 1: Surface Reading (What the author says)
Tick volume accumulates upticks and downticks to measure money flow. While historically powerful for tracking institutions via block trades, decimalization has damaged its reliability.

Layer 2: Structural Reading (How the author thinks)
The author uses a "rise and fall" narrative arc. He introduces a powerful concept, praises its historical effectiveness, and then methodically dismantles its current utility using modern market realities.

Layer 3: Contextual Reading (The Zeitgeist)
This chapter is a eulogy for the era of the human tape reader. It marks the precise boundary where the market transitioned from a human-readable flow of discrete transactions to an opaque, machine-dominated continuous function.

Layer 4: Generative Reading (The Next Evolution)
If microscopic data (ticks) is noisy and manipulated, we must zoom out to macroscopic data. This perfectly sets up the premise for Chapter 16: using volume to weight price over broader, smoothed trends where institutional footprints cannot be hidden.

CH 15 · IMPLEMENTATION

Surviving the Algo Era

Implementation Guide: Surviving the Algo Era

Phase 1: Recognize the Noise (Week 1)
- Pull up a 1-tick chart of a highly liquid stock like AAPL or SPY.
- Watch the rapid-fire execution. Notice how block trades are virtually non-existent, replaced by a blur of 100-share lots.
- Acknowledge that running standard Tick Accumulation on this data will yield a random walk.

Phase 2: Shift to Smoothed Horizons (Week 2-4)
- Abandon purely intraday, trade-by-trade cumulative indicators unless trading illiquid microcaps where algos don't play.
- Transition your analysis from "Did this trade happen on an uptick?" to "Did this entire 30-minute bar close strong on high volume?"

Phase 3: The Modern Alternative (Month 2+)
- Read Pascal Willain's Value in Time (recommended by the author). Ch 15, p. 156
- Begin tracking "Effective Volume" or look toward the Volume-Weighted Price Indicators discussed in the upcoming chapters.

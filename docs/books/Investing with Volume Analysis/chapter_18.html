CH 18 · THE BIG IDEA
"Price indexes show the surface trend, but market breadth reveals the actual liquidity and conviction beneath it."
A rising market with shrinking breadth is a warning of illiquidity and institutional distribution.
CH 18 · SUMMARY

Chapter Summary

In One Sentence: Market breadth indicators measure the internal strength of a trend by tracking the broad participation of individual issues, revealing institutional footprints before price tops.

The Setup: Price indexes can be skewed by cap-weighting, allowing a few large stocks to artificially support the market while underlying equities break down.

The Argument: True market health is determined by widespread participation (liquidity). When breadth diverges from the price index, a reversal is often imminent.

The Evidence: Historical divergences, such as the A-D line peaking in 1998 while the Tech Bubble pushed the composite higher until 2000, and the divergence of new highs in 2007.

The Controversy: Whether to include non-operating issues (ETFs, preferred stocks, bond funds) in breadth calculations, as these represent over 40% of NYSE listings today.

CH 18 · KEY TAKEAWAYS

Critical Insights

  • Breadth precedes price in major reversals

    Why it matters: Broad participation shrinks before the cap-weighted index rolls over.

    Evidence: The A-D line peaked in May 1928, over a year before the 1929 crash. Ch 18, p. 214

    Application: Use breadth as an early warning system to reduce long exposure.

  • Institutions distribute smaller caps first

    Why it matters: Large players must exit illiquid positions slowly while maintaining the illusion of a strong market through big-cap support.

    Evidence: Divergent paths of small caps versus large caps at market tops. Ch 18, p. 212

    Caveat: Divergence can last for years (e.g., 1998-2000) before the actual price collapse.

  • Decimalization altered historical baselines

    Why it matters: Moving from fractions to pennies drastically reduced the buying pressure needed to register a stock as an "advancer."

    Caveat: Modern breadth data is skewed; moving a stock now requires one-sixth the capital it did pre-2001. Ch 18, p. 216

CH 18 · MENTAL MODELS

Model 1: Breadth Divergence

Core Principle: A market trend must be supported by the majority of its participants; if the index climbs while the majority of stocks fall, the trend is starving for liquidity.

How It Works: Large-cap stocks disproportionately pull the index up, masking the weakness in small and mid-cap stocks. Because small-caps are less liquid, smart money sells them first when anticipating a downturn.

When to Apply:

  • Late-stage bull markets to detect internal exhaustion.
  • During multi-year rallies to distinguish healthy corrections from structural tops.

Limitations: Breadth divergence is a condition, not a timing signal. A market can have "bad breadth" for months or years before correcting.

CH 18 · MENTAL MODELS

Model 2: Arms Index (TRIN)

Core Principle: Tracks not just how many stocks are moving, but whether the volume is flowing toward the advancers or the decliners.

How It Works: Calculates the ratio of (Advancing Issues / Declining Issues) divided by (Advancing Volume / Declining Volume). An inverse indicator.

Interpretation:

  • < 1.0: Bullish. Up-volume is disproportionately large compared to the number of advancing issues.
  • 1.0: Equilibrium.
  • > 1.0: Bearish. Down-volume is disproportionately large compared to declining issues.

Limitations: Best used for short-term/intraday timing. Because it is calculated logarithmically, averaging the TRIN arithmetically creates a severe downward bias. Ch 18, p. 222

CH 18 · CONTEXT & BACKGROUND

Rocket Science Meets Wall Street

The Era: Late 1960s, prior to widespread personal computing.

The Innovation: Peter N. Haurlan, a JPL rocket scientist working on unmanned space probes, realized that the Exponential Moving Averages (EMAs) used to track missile guidance could filter stock market breadth data.

The Execution: Sneaking computer time on JPL mainframes at night, Haurlan introduced EMAs to the stock market. Sherman and Marian McClellan then developed the McClellan Oscillator in 1968 by subtracting the 39-day EMA from the 19-day EMA of the A-D line.

Modern Evolution: This aerospace mathematical framework solved the analog computation limits of the 1960s and formed the basis for modern momentum oscillators.

CH 18 · AUTHOR'S WARNINGS

Breadth Data Impurities

Beware the downward bias of delisted companies
Why: Cumulative indicators (like the A-D line) absorb the negative history of bankrupt companies. Once a company goes under, its past declines remain permanently encoded in the cumulative line. Focus on present divergences. Ch 18, p. 215
Do not assume all NYSE issues are common stocks
Why: Over 40% of NYSE issues today are ETFs, preferred stocks, or closed-end bond funds. Breadth statistics are heavily polluted by interest-rate-sensitive instruments. Ch 18, p. 217
Do not arithmetically average the Arms Index (TRIN)
Why: It's a logarithmic ratio. Averaging TRIN creates a downward mathematical bias. Use anti-logs to smooth the data. Ch 18, p. 222

CH 18 · COMPETING VIEWS

The Data Purity Debate

The Debate: Should market breadth include non-operating companies (ETFs, preferreds, closed-end funds)?

View 1: Purified Operating Companies Only (Paul Desmond / Lowery Research)
Claim: Non-operating companies distort the data. By stripping them out, you get a pure equity signal. This method successfully predicted the Fall 2001 market decline while traditional data was bullish. Ch 18, p. 217

View 2: Include Everything (Tom McClellan)
Claim: Non-operating securities, particularly preferred stocks, are heavily institutionally owned and highly interest-rate sensitive. They provide an excellent gauge of overall market liquidity. Their inclusion actually adds predictive power and reduces whipsaws. Ch 18, p. 218

CH 18 · DEEP DIVE

The Illusion of Cap-Weighting

Core Argument: The headline price index is a lie of proportion. Large cap stocks can prop up a market while the vast majority of companies are technically broken.

Logical Structure:

  1. Institutional investors manage portfolios too large to exit quickly without collapsing prices.
  2. To raise cash, they secretly liquidate less liquid small and mid-cap stocks first.
  3. They hold or gently support mega-caps to keep the index elevated and prevent panic.
  4. Breadth breaks down (bad internals) while the index price remains high (good externals).

Practical Implication: Never trust a new high in the S&P 500 if the number of new 52-week highs is contracting. Ch 18, p. 212

CH 18 · EVIDENCE EVALUATION

Negative Volume Index (NVI)

Claim: "NVI proved to be the most valuable of all breadth indexes" (Paul F. Dysart, Jr., 1967). Ch 18, p. 219

How it Works: NVI only updates on days when volume is lower than the previous day. It measures what the "smart money" is doing on quiet days, ignoring the retail-driven high-volume noise.

Quality Assessment: ⭐⭐⭐☆☆ (3/5)

Weaknesses: NVI lost its effectiveness in the mid-1960s due to the massive expansion in the number of listed issues.

Modern Adaptation: John A. Carder substituted exchange volume with Dow Jones Industrial volume to rectify the scaling issue, demonstrating that raw 1930s formulas must be continuously adapted to structural market changes.

CH 18 · IMPLEMENTATION

Applying Breadth Indicators

Phase 1: Understanding
Observe the daily Advance-Decline line alongside the S&P 500. Identify the days when they move in opposite directions.

Phase 2: Experimentation
Add the McClellan Oscillator (Ratio Adjusted) to your charting software. Note market behavior when the oscillator drops below -500 (oversold) and turns up. Ch 18, p. 224

Phase 3: Integration
Before taking any swing trade in an index ETF, check if the TRIN confirms the intraday trend. A rising market with a TRIN > 1.2 is a major warning.

Common Pitfall: Do not use breadth divergence for exact timing. A divergence is a condition indicating illiquidity, but an illiquid bubble can float higher for longer than you can remain solvent.

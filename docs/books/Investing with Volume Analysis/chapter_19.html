CH 19 · MENTAL MODELS

Mental Models

The "Blue Chip to Cow Chip" Phenomenon

When massive market-cap companies crash in price, their trading volume skyrockets due to massive float and penny-stock pricing, allowing them to disproportionately skew traditional aggregate index volume.
When to Apply:
  • During and after major market crashes (e.g., 2008 Financial Crisis).
  • When analyzing broad market volume metrics (like NYSE or S&P 500 total volume).
⚠️ Limitations:
  • Does not apply to price-weighted indices (like the Dow Jones) as directly as it does to broad volume tallies.
  • Requires tracking individual components to identify the 'cow chips'.

CH 19 · DEEP DIVE

Core Argument & The 'Cow Chips' Problem

Core Argument & The "Cow Chips" Problem

The central thesis is that one share traded does not equal one share traded when measuring the health of the entire market. If a $60 stock trades 1 million shares, that is $60 million of capital flow. If a $1 stock trades 10 million shares, that is only $10 million of capital flow.

Exxon (The Blue Chip)

  • Pre-crisis: $90, 3.8% S&P weighting.
  • Post-crisis: $60, 3.0% S&P weighting.
  • Volume: ~50 million shares.
  • Result: Huge price influence, relatively tiny volume influence.

Citigroup (The Cow Chip)

  • Pre-crisis: $50, 1.7% S&P weighting.
  • Post-crisis: $3.65, 0.79% S&P weighting.
  • Volume: 238 million shares.
  • Result: Tiny price influence, massive volume influence (over 3% of index volume).

CH 19 · DEEP DIVE

The 6-Step Methodology

The 6-Step Methodology

How to actually calculate Capital-Weighted Volume for an index:

  1. Calculate Market Capital: Stock Price × Shares Outstanding.
  2. Calculate Index Market Cap: Sum of all members' Market Capital.
  3. Calculate Component Weighting: Stock's Market Cap / Index Market Cap.
  4. Calculate Cap-Weighted Volume: Component Weighting × Stock Volume.
  5. Calculate Index Cap-Weighted Volume: Sum of all Cap-Weighted Volumes.
  6. Adjust for Price Change: Back out the daily price index change to normalize the data.
💡
Limitation Note: For long-term tracking, accumulated totals should not exceed one year without adjustment, because the secular growth in broad volume over time will distort the data.

CH 19 · COMPETING VIEWS

Competing Views

The Author's View: Cap-Weighted Volume

Core Claim: You must calculate the index's volume from the bottom-up, applying the exact market-cap weight to each component's daily share volume to get true participation.

Advantage: Shows exactly where the buying/selling originates (large caps vs. small caps) and perfectly aligns volume influence with price influence.

Alternative View: Dollar-Volume ($ Volume)

Core Claim: Modern quants argue that calculating Price × Volume (Dollar Volume) for each stock and summing it up is computationally simpler and achieves the exact same goal of neutralizing penny-stock distortions.

Advantage: Much easier to calculate, natively supported by most coding libraries (Pandas) and modern scanners without needing a 6-step weighting process.

Synthesis: The author was conceptually right—share volume is fundamentally broken. Whether you use his Cap-Weighted mathematical formula or the modern Dollar-Volume approach, the lesson remains: never trust raw aggregate share volume.

Implementation Guide

  • Phase 1: Awareness
    Stop looking at "NYSE Total Volume" or "$VOLI" on your charts. Treat them as broken clocks.
  • Phase 2: Platform Audit
    Check if your trading platform (ThinkOrSwim, TradingView, TC2000) offers Cap-Weighted Volume or Dollar-Volume natively.
  • Phase 3: Coding/Switching
    If it's not available natively, code a custom indicator. On TradingView, a simple proxy for an index is to sum the (close * volume) of the top 10 weighted components.
⚠️
Common Pitfall: Don't try to calculate this manually in Excel every day. You will burn out. Automate it or switch to a platform that supports it.

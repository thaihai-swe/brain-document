CH 20 · THE BIG IDEA

The Big Idea

CH 20 · THE BIG IDEA
"The best offense is a good defense."
Success isn't just about picking winners; it's about minimizing the mathematical devastation of losers through strict position sizing and volume-adjusted stops.
CH 20 · CONTEXT & BACKGROUND

Context & Background

Historical Moment

Written after the "lost decade" of equities (2000-2010) where buy-and-hold failed dramatically. Dormeier cites the devastating sequence-of-returns risk for retirees who believed that standard index investing was entirely safe. p. 246

Intellectual Lineage

Builds directly upon Harry Markowitz's Modern Portfolio Theory (1990 Nobel Prize). Markowitz proved that inversely correlated assets reduce risk while increasing return. Dormeier extends this by incorporating volume dynamics into the asset allocation and exit rules.

Author's Lens

As a risk-averse portfolio manager, Dormeier treats risk management as a structural necessity, not a theoretical abstraction. He views market losses as mathematically asymmetrical (e.g., a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover).

Then vs. Now (2026)

The critique of "buy and hope" remains fiercely relevant in modern algorithms and zero-day options trading, where drawdowns happen faster, making systematic stops (like AVSL) even more critical today.

CH 20 · MENTAL MODELS

Mental Models

The "Sports Team" Position Allocation

Manage your portfolio's "salary cap" by categorizing assets into roles and strict allocations. p. 249
How It Works:
  • All-Stars (40%): Highest conviction, reliable ideas (e.g., 5% position sizes x 8 stocks).
  • Starters (30%): Core holdings (e.g., 3% x 10 stocks).
  • Rookies (15%): Unproven but high potential (e.g., 1.5% x 10 stocks).
  • Disabled List (15%): Underperforming stocks that are on probation.

The Gardener

"A gardener begins by sowing seeds... picks the weeds and waters flowers just as the savvy investor sells losers and reinvests in winning positions." p. 256
Application: Realizing a loss is not an admission of ultimate defeat; it is the deliberate freeing up of capital (pulling weeds) to reallocate to stronger assets (watering flowers).
CH 20 · AUTHOR'S WARNINGS

Author's Warnings

Beware the "Buy and Hope" Strategy
Why: The mutual fund industry promotes "don't miss the 10 best days," but deliberately ignores that missing the 10 worst days dramatically increases returns. Passive holding without risk management exposes capital to catastrophic compounding math. p. 244-245
Do not average down to meet income needs
Why: Sequence of returns risk. If a retiree takes a 5% draw during a bear market, they are forced to sell more shares at lower prices. A $1M portfolio taking 5% draws from 2000-2010 dropped to $297k. p. 246
Do not over-weight positions based on "Luck"
Why: Placing too much capital in one stock means you are placing faith in that stock rather than your process. Limit position sizes so that bad luck on one stock doesn't destroy the portfolio. p. 248
CH 20 · NUMBERS & STUDIES

The Math of Compounding

The mathematical realities of drawdowns reveal why capital preservation is the most critical function of a portfolio manager. Losses compound downward aggressively. p. 253

11%
Gain needed
To recover from a 10% loss. The math is relatively symmetrical early on.
25%
Gain needed
To recover from a 20% loss. The asymmetry begins to accelerate rapidly.
100%
Gain needed
To recover from a 50% loss. A catastrophic drawdown requires doubling your money just to break even.
📊
Missing the Best vs. Worst Days (1984-1998): Missing the 10 best days dropped annualized returns from 17.89% to 14.24%. But missing BOTH the 10 best AND 10 worst days boosted returns to over 20.31%. Risk management (missing the worst) is mathematically superior to buy-and-hold. p. 244
CH 20 · SIGNAL MATRIX

AVSL Decision Rules

The Anti-Volume Stop Loss (AVSL) dynamically adjusts based on a security's individual volatility (Bollinger Bands) and its volume health (VPCI). p. 254

Volume Confirming Trend (High VPCI) Volume Contradicting Trend (Low VPCI)
High Volatility (e.g. Tech/Growth) Widest LeashHigh volatility requires room to breathe; strong volume provides confidence to allow normal fluctuations. Tighten StopHigh volatility without volume backing means risk is elevated. Pull stops up to protect capital.
Low Volatility (e.g. Consumer Staples) Standard LeashTight baseline stop is slightly loosened to ride the confirmed low-volatility uptrend. Tightest LeashStable stocks losing volume support have no excuse for large drops. Stop out quickly.
💡
AVSL Formula Concept: AVSL uses the Lower Bollinger Band of the price's lows, modified by Volume Price Confirmation Indicator (VPCI) and Volume Multiplier (VM).

CH 20 · DEEP DIVE

The 4D Allocation Process

Dormeier's Four-Dimensional Strategic Allocation seeks to achieve maximum diversification by blending structural portfolio parameters with dynamic performance catalysts. p. 248-251

1
Position Allocation
Sizing limits the damage any single issuer can do. Cap positions (e.g., max 5%) to ensure performance relies on your process, not luck.
2
Horizontal Allocation
Diversifying across the 10 major S&P sectors and geographic regions. Use top-down target ranges (e.g., 10-15% industrials).
3
Vertical Allocation
Allocating among market capitalizations (mega, large, mid, small, micro). Dynamically shift weightings as macro environments change.
4
Performance Drivers
Fusing multiple catalysts (e.g., VARSITI relative strength + oversold condition) so the portfolio doesn't rely entirely on one indicator.
CH 20 · COMPETING VIEWS

Passive Indexing vs. Dynamic Risk

Efficient Market / Passive Indexing

Core Claim: The market is efficient and always goes up over long horizons. Active management is a waste of fees.

Approach: Buy index funds, hold forever, ignore volatility, "ride the rollercoaster with seatbelt fastened."

Fatal Flaw: Ignores "Sequence of Returns" risk. If you need income during a secular bear market (like 2000-2010), forced selling near the bottom permanently destroys capital. p. 245-246

Dynamic Allocation & Risk Management (Dormeier)

Core Claim: Buy-and-hold is actually "Buy-and-Hope." Capital preservation is paramount because mathematical drawdowns are asymmetrical.

Approach: Use AVSL (Anti-Volume Stop Loss) and 4D allocation. Avoid the worst days by ruthlessly stopping out of failing assets.

Advantage: Ensures survival. "Good investors are good winners, while great investors are great losers" (surviving to invest another day).

CH 20 · KEY TAKEAWAYS

Key Takeaways

  • 1
    Exits dictate investment outcomes, not entries.
    Why: Once you buy, you lose control to the market. Your only remaining control is the exit. Application: Define your AVSL or hard stop before entering the trade. p. 252
  • 2
    Volatility must dictate stop width.
    Why: A flat 7% stop on Coca-Cola might take a year to hit, trapping dead money. A 7% stop on Google might trigger intraday due to noise. Application: Use volatility-adjusted trailing stops (like Bollinger band logic) to customize the leash. p. 254
  • 3
    The best mistakes are small and quickly realized.
    Why: Humans are conservative with winners and aggressive with losers, holding out "hope" for a rebound. Caveat: Realizing losses takes discipline; you must override the ego's desire to be "right." p. 256
  • 4
    Avoid sequence of returns risk.
    Why: Averages lie. Earning an average 8% over 10 years means nothing if you suffer a massive drawdown in year 1 while drawing income. Application: Active risk management is essential for anyone supplementing income from their portfolio. p. 246
CH 20 · IMPLEMENTATION GUIDE

Applying Dynamic Defense

Phase 1: Rules
Phase 2: Roster
Phase 3: 4D Mapping
Phase 4: Pruning

Phase 1: Establish Exit Rules (Week 1)

  • Calculate the historical volatility for every asset in your portfolio.
  • Set an automated trailing stop (like AVSL or Chandelier exit) for each holding based on its volatility and volume strength.

Phase 2: Build the Roster (Week 2)

  • Categorize your holdings: All-Stars (max 40%), Starters (max 30%), Rookies (15%), Disabled List (15%).
  • Size positions accordingly (e.g., 5% max for All-Stars, 1.5% for Rookies).

Phase 3: Apply 4D Allocation (Month 2)

  • Check horizontal exposure (are you over-indexed on Tech?).
  • Check vertical exposure (Large vs Small cap).

Phase 4: Objective Pruning (Ongoing)

  • When an asset hits its AVSL stop, sell it immediately. Do not rationalize. Free up capital for new "rookies" or to water the "all-stars."
CH 20 · CLOSING ACTIONS

Closing Actions

  • Calculate your mathematical recovery needs.
    Review your current unrealized losses. If any position is down more than 20%, recognize that it needs a 25% gain just to break even. Stop hoping and re-evaluate objectively.
  • Implement hard stops on the "Disabled List."
    Identify the bottom 15% of your portfolio's performers. Place tight, non-negotiable trailing stops beneath them today to force capital reallocation if they fail to perform.
  • Cap your maximum position size.
    No matter your conviction, determine an absolute maximum portfolio weight (e.g., 5% or 8%) per issuer. Enforce this rule to protect your process from bad luck.
